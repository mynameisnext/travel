Travel A Lot

Everyone thinks they know what is really healthy - vegetables and fruits rich in vitamins, low in fat and, above all, no animal fats, and whole grain products. After all, that corresponds to what the state-funded German Nutrition Society recommends. But what is the scientific basis for this advice? Numerous researchers say: on none.

Peter Nawroth from Heidelberg University Hospital considers the many recommendations for whole grains, fat and vitamins to be nonsense: "Basically we don't know exactly how much is needed. But we don't have to. Because apart from very seriously ill people, for example cancer patients "Nobody in our country suffers from vitamin deficiencies. What really matters to your health is the amount you eat. It makes you sick if you overeat. What you eat doesn't really matter."

Better healthy than tasty

But you know what you ate. Most of the time, people just ask what was eaten afterwards. The only really good study, with a large number of participants and spanning eight years, showed that it did not matter how the subjects ate. Cancer, heart attack, stroke, diabetes - all the same. "
